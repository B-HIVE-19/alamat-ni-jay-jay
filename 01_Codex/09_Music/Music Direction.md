## Directional Intent

The score should support the emotional sweep of a classic JRPG while honoring the cultural texture and atmosphere of the game world.

Music should carry memory, place, danger, warmth, and wonder. The score should help players remember companions, villages, dungeons, legends, and emotional turning points long after they stop playing.

This document defines musical philosophy, not individual songs.

## Music Director Mandate

Music must become emotionally tied to locations and memories.

The player should not only remember what happened in a place; they should remember how that place felt when its theme began.

## Musical Pillars

1. Memorable themes over background filler.
2. Emotional authenticity over spectacle.
3. Filipino-inspired texture without pastiche.
4. Strong place identity for villages, regions, and dungeons.
5. Motifs that support family, friendship, courage, loyalty, legends, and adventure.
6. Dungeon music that reinforces curiosity, isolation, risk, discovery, and triumph.
7. Music should make memory playable.

## Instrumentation

Instrumentation should be researched and intentional. Filipino-inspired sounds, rhythms, or textures should be transformed into original fantasy music rather than copied superficially.

Instrumentation should suggest material, place, weather, ritual, and memory. A village, dungeon, legend, and character should not feel interchangeable.

## Motifs

Motifs should help the player feel continuity across characters, places, memories, legends, and emotional growth.

Motif rules:

- Motifs should evolve with character arcs.
- Location motifs may return in altered form after story changes.
- Legend motifs should feel old, partial, or remembered differently by region.
- The tribute spirit should be protected through sincerity, restraint, and warmth.
- Motifs should not over-explain emotion; they should invite feeling.

## Opening Theme

The opening theme should establish wonder, invitation, emotional sincerity, and the promise of a grand journey.

It should not begin with empty spectacle. It should feel like a door opening: personal, adventurous, and larger than the player understands.

Emotional function:

- Introduce the heart of Alamat.
- Suggest adventure before explaining it.
- Carry warmth beneath mystery.
- Establish musical DNA that can return later in transformed form.

## Village Themes

Village themes should make settlements feel lived-in before the player arrives.

They should express daily rhythm, weather, labor, gossip, family, local pride, local grief, and regional identity. A fishing village, terrace village, market village, shrine village, and river crossing should not share the same emotional texture.

Emotional function:

- Safety without emptiness.
- Warmth with history.
- Community before quest utility.
- A feeling that people continue living when the player leaves.

## Dungeon Themes

Dungeon themes should support first-person exploration: curiosity, isolation, risk, discovery, and triumph.

They should not merely loop ominous ambience. A dungeon theme should tell the player that the space has history and intent.

Emotional function:

- Pull the player deeper.
- Make silence and sound meaningful.
- Reinforce landmarks, secrets, and danger.
- Shift subtly as the player understands the dungeon.

Dungeon music may use restraint, repetition, distant motifs, environmental rhythm, muffled percussion, breath-like textures, or fragments of old melodies to imply memory and unease.

## Battle Music

Battle music should create momentum while preserving adventure and readability.

It should energize the player without flattening every encounter into the same emotional state. Standard battles should feel dangerous but not constantly apocalyptic.

Emotional function:

- Create urgency.
- Support turn-based decision-making.
- Preserve party courage.
- Keep the adventure moving.

## Boss Music

Boss music should express identity, not just intensity.

A boss theme should reflect the boss encounter's story function, dungeon identity, creature ecology, emotional stakes, or character pressure.

Emotional function:

- Signal that the confrontation matters.
- Tie the boss to place, legend, or character.
- Build pressure without losing musical clarity.
- Make victory feel earned.

## Victory Theme

The victory theme should feel like relief, courage, and forward motion.

It should not be so large that it breaks tone after ordinary battles. It should reward the player while preserving the larger journey.

Emotional function:

- Release tension.
- Affirm survival.
- Encourage momentum.
- Allow later variations for major victories.

## Campfire Theme

The campfire theme should support intimacy, humor, vulnerability, and party attachment.

It should feel like a small circle of safety in a large and dangerous world.

Emotional function:

- Let companions feel human.
- Support quiet conversation.
- Make warmth memorable.
- Create contrast with dungeons and danger.

## Sad Moments

Sad music should be restrained, sincere, and earned.

It should not manipulate the player into feeling. It should create room for grief, regret, tenderness, memory, and silence.

Emotional function:

- Honor emotional truth.
- Protect the tribute foundation.
- Leave space for the player to feel.
- Avoid melodrama unless the scene has earned it.

## Legend Themes

Legend themes should feel older than the present story.

They may appear as fragments, songs, ritual phrases, regional variations, dungeon echoes, or motifs hidden inside other themes.

Emotional function:

- Suggest that myths contain truth.
- Connect villages, ruins, dungeons, and characters.
- Make old stories feel alive.
- Reward players who recognize returning motifs.

## Ending Theme

The ending theme should resolve the emotional journey without closing the world completely.

It should carry affection, wonder, memory, and the feeling that the adventure mattered.

Emotional function:

- Honor Jay Jay's journey.
- Resolve party attachment.
- Preserve the tribute spirit.
- Leave the player with warmth and remembrance.

## Area Themes

Area themes should make locations feel inhabited, historical, and emotionally specific.

Each major area theme should define:

- Place identity.
- Climate and time of day.
- Cultural texture.
- Emotional function.
- Connection to nearby legends, dungeons, or characters.

## Silence

Silence is part of the score.

Use silence or near-silence for tension, grief, sacred spaces, strange ruins, aftermath, and moments where the player should listen to the world.

Silence should be intentional, not empty.

## Music Approval Tests

Before approving a musical direction, ask:

1. Does this reinforce the identity of Alamat?
2. Does this make a place, character, legend, or memory more emotionally specific?
3. Does this avoid generic fantasy scoring?
4. Does this support the player's current emotional state without overexplaining it?
5. Could this theme return later with new meaning?
6. Will players remember how this moment felt?
