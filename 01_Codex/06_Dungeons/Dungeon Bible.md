## Dungeon Design Mandate

Dungeons should combine exploration, atmosphere, puzzle logic, combat pacing, and story revelation in a way that supports the larger journey.

The original *Phantasy Star* is the primary inspiration for dungeon exploration feel: mystery, danger, adventure, disorientation, discovery, and the satisfaction of learning a hostile space. *Alamat ni Jay Jay* should not copy those dungeons, maps, settings, enemies, or story concepts. The goal is to recreate the emotional charge of first-person exploration through original environments shaped by Filipino landscapes, local history, and folklore.

No dungeon may be treated as a generic cave, ruin, forest path, temple, mine, sewer, or tower. Every dungeon must have an identity strong enough that players remember it by what happened there, what it felt like to explore, and what the place revealed about the world.

No dungeon may exist simply as a gameplay obstacle. Every dungeon must tell a story.

Every dungeon should become memorable.

## Emotional Target

First-person dungeons should recreate the emotional experience of classic JRPG exploration without copying specific layouts or designs.

Every major dungeon should evoke:

| Emotion | Dungeon Expression |
| --- | --- |
| Wonder | The player enters a place that feels older, stranger, or larger than expected. |
| Curiosity | Unanswered questions, visible secrets, odd landmarks, and local legends pull the player deeper. |
| Risk | Navigation, resources, encounters, traps, uncertainty, and retreat pressure create meaningful danger. |
| Isolation | The first-person view, sound design, distance from safety, and limited information make the player feel alone inside the place. |
| Discovery | Exploration reveals shortcuts, hidden rooms, lore, treasure, ecology, or truths inside legends. |
| Triumph | Understanding the dungeon, defeating its boss, opening its exit, or returning safely should feel earned. |

## Dungeon Approval Test

Before approval, every dungeon must answer:

1. What story does this dungeon tell?
2. Why does this place exist in the world?
3. What happened here before Jay Jay arrived?
4. What legend points toward the truth of this place?
5. What lives here, and why?
6. Who built it, altered it, damaged it, claimed it, or avoided it?
7. What architecture, construction logic, layout, or landmark makes it visually and spatially memorable?
8. How do lighting and music shape the player's emotional state?
9. What does the player learn by exploring instead of rushing to the exit?
10. What secret rewards curiosity?
11. What emotional transformation should the player feel from entrance to exit?
12. What map philosophy supports discovery, risk, and triumph?
13. If combat were removed, would this dungeon still be worth exploring?

## Dungeon Structure

Every dungeon document must include:

| Section | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Purpose | Why this dungeon exists in the game and in the world. |
| Legend | What people believe about this place, whether true or distorted. |
| History | What happened here before the player arrived. |
| Construction | Who built it, what methods and materials were used, and how time changed it. |
| Architecture | Built forms, materials, thresholds, chambers, ruins, and spatial identity. |
| Layout | First-person navigation structure, loops, landmarks, hazards, and spatial tricks. |
| Exploration Loops | Optional paths, return routes, shortcuts, locked loops, and risk-reward branches. |
| Memorable Landmarks | Visual, acoustic, ritual, architectural, or ecological anchors that help players remember the space. |
| Atmosphere | Sensory mood, lighting, weather, sound, texture, and emotional pressure. |
| Lighting | Visibility, shadow, color temperature, lantern logic, natural light, magical light, darkness, and guidance. |
| Environmental Storytelling | Evidence of past lives, conflict, rituals, disasters, or daily use. |
| Music | Musical identity, instrumentation, pacing, silence, tension, and transitions. |
| Puzzle Design | Core interactions, rules, clues, failure states, and escalation. |
| Creature Ecology | Why creatures are here, how they survive, and how they interact. |
| Secret Areas | Optional spaces that reward curiosity and careful mapping. |
| Boss Encounter | Climactic conflict rooted in the dungeon's identity. |
| Treasure | Items placed with narrative, emotional, and mechanical intention. |
| Shortcuts | Opened routes, return paths, retreat options, and mastery rewards. |
| Map Philosophy | How the player should understand, remember, and navigate the space. |
| Player Emotions | Intended emotional movement from entrance to exit. |
| Reward | Treasure, knowledge, shortcuts, character moments, abilities, access, or emotional payoff. |
| Exit Strategy | How the player leaves, returns, shortcuts, aftermath, and recovery flow. |

## Exploration Philosophy

Exploration should be more than reaching the exit. Dungeons should encourage players to map mentally, notice patterns, test suspicions, take risks, and feel the relief of finding a shortcut or safe return path.

Good dungeon exploration should create questions:

1. What is this place?
2. Who used it before?
3. What went wrong here?
4. What is watching or waiting here?
5. What am I willing to risk to see what is around the next turn?

## First-Person Rules

First-person dungeon exploration should create intimacy with space. Corners, doorways, stairways, dead ends, sound cues, and landmarks matter because the player experiences the dungeon from inside it, not above it.

Layouts should support:

- Recognizable landmarks.
- Meaningful loops and shortcuts.
- Optional branches with clear risk and reward.
- Occasional disorientation without unfair confusion.
- Environmental clues that reward attention.
- Exit planning and retreat tension.

## Map Philosophy

Maps should support the feeling of first-person exploration rather than erase it. A dungeon map is a tool for memory, confidence, and mastery, not a replacement for attention.

Map design should decide:

- Whether the player has no map, a partial map, a purchased map, an unlockable map, or an auto-filled map.
- Whether landmarks, sounds, lighting, and construction details are enough for orientation.
- How shortcuts reduce friction without removing danger.
- How secrets remain discoverable after the main route is understood.
- How dead ends feel purposeful rather than empty.
- How the boss route, treasure route, and exit route relate to each other.

## Exploration Loops

Dungeon layouts should encourage exploration rather than simple exit-finding.

Useful loop types include:

| Loop Type | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Landmark Loop | Brings the player back to a recognizable anchor with new understanding. |
| Shortcut Loop | Opens a safer route after risk, puzzle-solving, or boss progress. |
| Secret Loop | Rewards careful observation with hidden rooms, lore, treasure, or alternate paths. |
| Resource Loop | Lets players decide whether to press forward or retreat with limited supplies. |
| Legend Loop | Recontextualizes a local myth after the player finds evidence inside the dungeon. |
| Ecology Loop | Shows how creatures move, nest, feed, flee, or claim territory. |

## Architecture

Dungeon architecture should reveal purpose and history. A dungeon's walls, thresholds, floors, ceilings, water systems, carvings, bridges, chambers, and collapsed sections should explain how the place was used before it became dangerous.

Architecture should support navigation through memorable spatial identity: a flooded hall, a root-pierced shrine, a tide gate, a spiral stair, a lantern corridor, a broken observatory, a carved ancestor wall, or a chamber where sound behaves strangely.

## Construction

Construction details should make a dungeon feel physically and historically real.

Every dungeon should define:

- Original builders or natural formation.
- Materials and construction methods.
- Original use.
- Later repairs, occupations, damage, collapse, flooding, ritual alteration, or creature modification.
- What parts are ancient, recent, sacred, stolen, improvised, or unstable.

Construction should affect gameplay: blocked passages, weak floors, hidden voids, climbable structures, sound travel, water flow, light shafts, trap logic, and shortcuts.

## Lighting

Lighting is part of dungeon design, not decoration. It should guide attention, create fear, reveal history, hide secrets, and support first-person orientation.

Lighting sources may include:

- Sun shafts.
- Torches or lanterns.
- Bioluminescent plants or creatures.
- Reflected water.
- Volcanic glow.
- Ritual candles.
- Ancient mechanisms.
- Magical or spiritual light.
- Total darkness used sparingly and fairly.

Lighting should help define the emotional rhythm of the dungeon: invitation, uncertainty, dread, discovery, relief, and triumph.

## Puzzle Philosophy

Puzzles should feel like they belong to the place. A puzzle should not appear to be a detached lock-and-key challenge unless the fiction explains why it exists.

Puzzle mechanics may involve local rituals, folk beliefs, environmental observation, sound, light, water, offerings, language, memory, creature behavior, or spatial navigation.

## Encounter Pacing

Turn-based battles should punctuate exploration rather than flatten it. Encounter pacing should protect the mood of the dungeon and make danger feel present without exhausting curiosity.

Creature placement should follow ecology and story logic. Enemies should not feel randomly poured into rooms.

## Reward Philosophy

Rewards should feel discovered, not dispensed. Rewards can include equipment, consumables, lore, shortcuts, optional scenes, music cues, character insight, crafting resources, access to future spaces, or a deeper understanding of the dungeon's story.

The best rewards satisfy curiosity and reinforce the dungeon's identity.

## Treasure Philosophy

Treasure should belong to the dungeon. It should feel stored, hidden, lost, offered, stolen, grown, guarded, buried, washed in, or left behind for a reason.

Treasure placement should answer:

- Who put this here?
- Why was it not taken before?
- What does it reveal about the dungeon?
- Why is it useful now?
- How does finding it reward curiosity?

## Dungeon Identity Test

Before a dungeon is approved, it must pass this test:

> If all enemies were removed, would this dungeon still be memorable?

If the answer is no, the dungeon needs stronger history, atmosphere, layout identity, environmental storytelling, secrets, or puzzle logic.

## Story Requirement

Every dungeon must tell a story through space. The story may be about a disaster, a broken promise, a forgotten ritual, a living ecology, a political crime, a family secret, a misunderstood creature, an ancient technology, a community's fear, or a legend that preserves truth.

The story should be understandable through exploration, not only dialogue.

## Memorable Dungeon Standard

A dungeon is not approved until the team can describe what players will remember:

- The legend that pulled them in.
- The landmark they used to orient themselves.
- The secret they almost missed.
- The creature behavior that made the place feel alive.
- The puzzle that belonged to the location.
- The boss encounter that expressed the dungeon's identity.
- The emotion they felt when they found the shortcut, treasure, or exit.

## Open Questions

- What is the target average length for early, mid-game, and late-game dungeons?
- Will dungeon maps be visible, unlockable, purchasable, hand-drawn by the player, or absent?
- How punishing should retreat and resource depletion feel?
- What is the default map philosophy for early dungeons?
- How dark can dungeons become before readability suffers?
