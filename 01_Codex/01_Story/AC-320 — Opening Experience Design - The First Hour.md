# AC-320 - Opening Experience Design

## The First Hour

**Project:** Alamat ni Jay Jay  
**Studio:** 1910 Creative Studios  
**Document Type:** Canonical opening experience design  
**Status:** Pending Creative Director Approval  
**Approved By:** Jeffrey Moralde  
**Canon Effective:** Upon Approval

---

## 1. Design Question

This document answers one central question:

**How do we make players fall in love with Alamat before asking them to save it?**

The first hour must make home feel alive, specific, warm, and worth remembering. The player should not begin by understanding the stakes of the world. The player should begin by feeling the shape of Jay Jay's life before adventure interrupts it.

The opening hour is not a tutorial in urgency. It is an invitation into belonging.

---

## 2. Core Intent

The opening of Alamat ni Jay Jay begins with life.

Not war.  
Not prophecy.  
Not tragedy.  
Not spectacle.

The player first experiences a peaceful coastal village at the beginning of a day. The world is already in motion before Jay Jay becomes the player's focus. People are working, laughing, cooking, preparing, calling to each other, sweeping thresholds, mending nets, moving through routines that feel older than the story itself.

The emotional contract is simple:

**This place matters before the plot says it matters.**

By the end of the first hour, the player should not feel pushed out of home. The player should feel reluctant to leave and quietly compelled to know what waits beyond the horizon.

---

## 3. Opening Philosophy

The first hour establishes a philosophy of curiosity rather than command.

The player should feel that exploration is not a task assigned from outside, but an extension of Jay Jay's way of being. Jay Jay notices things. He sketches. He listens. He remembers details. He pays attention to people others may pass by.

This is the foundation of his legend.

Jay Jay is not introduced as someone chosen by fate. He is introduced as someone who looks carefully at the world and, because of that attention, eventually sees what others miss.

The opening hour must protect the following principles:

- Home before adventure.
- Curiosity before danger.
- Wonder before spectacle.
- Characters before systems.
- Memory before mission.

---

## 4. Canon Opening Shot

The opening begins on a black screen.

There is no music.

Only natural sound:

- Gentle ocean waves.
- Wind moving through trees.
- Morning birds.
- A distant rooster.
- Village life slowly waking.

This moment should be held long enough for the player to hear the world before seeing it. The first emotional cue is trust: the game is willing to breathe.

The image fades in.

The first image is not Jay Jay's face. It is a pencil moving across paper.

The camera reveals a worn sketchbook filled with fragments of village life: boats, birds, plants, old shrines, baskets, faces, rooftops, cooking fires, shorelines, and small details that suggest years of looking closely.

The camera slowly pulls back.

Jay Jay sits beneath a tree overlooking the sea, sketching the sunrise.

This first image defines him as an observer before the player ever understands him as a hero.

---

## 5. Protected First Character Moment

A young child notices Jay Jay drawing and asks to see the sketchbook.

Jay Jay hesitates, embarrassed, then shares it.

The child compliments the drawing. Jay Jay deflects gently and awkwardly, looking toward the ocean.

This moment is protected canon.

Its purpose is to establish:

- Humility.
- Kindness.
- Creativity.
- Quiet confidence.
- Jay Jay's discomfort with praise.

No additional final dialogue should be written around this exchange in this document. The moment should remain small, unforced, and emotionally clean. It is not a joke setup. It is not a dramatic confession. It is a soft first proof that Jay Jay's inner life is generous and observant.

---

## 6. First-Hour Emotional Arc

The first hour should move through four emotional phases.

### Phase 1: Awakening

The world is heard before it is seen.

The player is placed into a morning that feels lived-in, not staged. The village is not waiting for Jay Jay to activate it. It already has rhythm, labor, humor, and affection.

The feeling is calm attention.

### Phase 2: Belonging

The player gradually understands that Jay Jay is known here.

People recognize him. Some tease him. Some trust him. Children are comfortable approaching him. Elders expect him to listen. Workers let him move through their spaces without suspicion.

The feeling is community.

### Phase 3: Wonder

The player begins noticing layers beneath ordinary life: old carvings near a shrine, stories embedded in songs, a pattern in the tide, birds circling farther out than expected, an elder pausing at something they do not explain.

Nothing should become urgent yet.

The feeling is curiosity.

### Phase 4: The Horizon

Near the end of the opening hour, Jay Jay looks beyond the sea and notices a barely visible island through the morning mist.

The image may be real, imagined, remembered, or impossible.

No explanation is given.

Jay Jay studies it, closes his sketchbook, and returns to the village.

The feeling is unresolved wonder.

---

## 7. First Ten Minutes

The first ten minutes should avoid formal quest pressure.

The player should be allowed to inhabit the village before being asked to accomplish anything. The goal is not to teach through instruction, but to teach through atmosphere and attention.

The player should naturally encounter:

- Neighbors beginning their day.
- Children playing and watching adults work.
- Fishermen preparing boats and nets.
- Food being cooked or shared.
- Market life beginning to assemble.
- Music emerging from ordinary spaces.
- Small jokes and familiar teasing.
- Quiet acts of kindness.

Jay Jay's identity should be revealed through how people respond to him, what he notices, and what he chooses to pause for.

The first ten minutes should feel like entering a memory rather than starting a mission.

---

## 8. The First Player Goal

The first player goal is exploration.

Not combat.  
Not efficiency.  
Not optimization.  
Not saving the world.

The opening should encourage the player to:

- Walk.
- Listen.
- Observe.
- Sketch.
- Discover.
- Become attached.

The player's early momentum should come from interest, not pressure. The village should reward attention with emotional texture: a sound, a smile, a remembered object, a small visual detail, a glimpse of daily life, a place that feels worth returning to.

---

## 9. Jay Jay In The First Hour

Jay Jay should be introduced through behavior rather than exposition.

He is:

- A quiet observer.
- A creative child of the village.
- Someone who sees beauty in ordinary things.
- Kind without performance.
- Curious before he is brave.
- Slightly awkward when seen too clearly.

The first hour should resist defining him as "destined." His importance should come from attention. He notices what others ignore, not because he is superior, but because he loves the world enough to look closely.

His sketchbook is the clearest symbol of this identity. It is not merely an inventory of drawings. It is evidence of relationship. Every page says: Jay Jay has been paying attention to home for a long time.

---

## 10. Home As Character

The village must feel like a character with its own pulse.

Home should not be represented only by architecture or scenic beauty. It should be defined by rituals, voices, labor, food, humor, memory, and repeated gestures.

The player should feel that if Jay Jay were not present, life would continue. This makes the world feel real. Jay Jay belongs to it, but he does not own it.

The village should communicate:

- Warmth without perfection.
- Community without sentimentality.
- History without exposition.
- Beauty without gloss.
- Humor without breaking tone.
- Spiritual presence without immediate explanation.

Home must be loved before it is endangered.

---

## 11. Cinematic Direction

The cinematic language of the first hour should be patient, grounded, and observational.

Camera behavior should favor:

- Slow reveals.
- Human-scale framing.
- Natural movement.
- Environmental detail.
- Faces in context rather than isolated hero shots.
- The sea as emotional background, not immediate threat.

The camera should not announce legend too early. Jay Jay is not framed as a chosen savior in the opening. He is framed as a boy sitting beneath a tree, drawing the morning because he cannot help but notice it.

The first hour should avoid aggressive visual punctuation. No sudden mythic iconography should seize the tone. Wonder should enter gradually through the ordinary.

---

## 12. Visual Identity

The opening hour should establish Alamat as vibrant, coastal, tactile, and alive.

The visual identity should lean into:

- Morning gold over water.
- Greens shaped by wind and salt air.
- Worn wood, woven fibers, stone, clay, cloth, and paper.
- Hand-touched surfaces.
- Shrines and old places that feel cared for, not museum-like.
- Movement in small things: leaves, smoke, birds, fabric, waves, hands at work.

The village should not look like a postcard. It should look lived in.

Beauty should come from specificity: a repaired fishing net, a child's chalk mark, cooking smoke crossing sunlight, a page corner softened by use, sandals at a doorway, a boat name painted by hand.

---

## 13. Audio Direction

The first sound of the game is the world itself.

The audio design should create intimacy before score.

Natural sound leads:

- Water.
- Wind.
- Birds.
- Rooster call.
- Footsteps.
- Distant voices.
- Morning work.
- Cooking.
- Wood, rope, paper, and pencil.

Music should enter carefully and feel like it rises from the place rather than being placed over it. The opening should earn its musical identity slowly.

Village music may be diegetic or near-diegetic in feeling: someone practicing, a rhythm from work, a melody hummed from another space. The score should not tell players how important the story is. It should help them feel how alive the morning is.

Silence is important. The black-screen opening and the horizon moment both require restraint.

---

## 14. Level Design Experience

The first-hour space should feel open enough to invite wandering and intimate enough to remain emotionally legible.

The village should be arranged around natural curiosity:

- The tree and sea overlook establish Jay Jay's inward life.
- The path back into the village reconnects him to community.
- The shore shows labor and horizon.
- The market shows rhythm and humor.
- Homes show belonging and detail.
- The shrine or old place hints at memory and myth.
- Higher or quieter vantage points create moments of reflection.

The player should not feel lost, but also should not feel dragged through a corridor of instructions.

The world should gently pull attention through sound, movement, color, and character presence.

---

## 15. Exploration Philosophy

Exploration in the opening hour is emotional, not extractive.

The player is not trained to strip the village for rewards. The player is invited to notice.

Discovery should feel like:

- Finding a place where someone has left a personal trace.
- Seeing Jay Jay recognize something familiar.
- Understanding relationships through repeated details.
- Noticing the old world beneath the present one.
- Catching a glimpse of something that may matter later.

The player's first relationship with Alamat should be attention, not acquisition.

---

## 16. Supporting Cast Introduction

The first hour should introduce supporting characters as part of village life, not as a lineup of future functions.

Characters should appear while doing something:

- Working.
- Cooking.
- Arguing lightly.
- Teaching.
- Repairing.
- Carrying.
- Singing.
- Watching the weather.
- Caring for a child.
- Remembering something.

The player should come away with emotional impressions before formal roles.

The important question is not "What does this person do for the game?" but "Why would Jay Jay remember this person when he is far from home?"

---

## 17. Humor And Warmth

Humor in the first hour should be local, affectionate, and character-based.

It should come from familiarity:

- People knowing Jay Jay too well.
- Adults teasing each other.
- Children being blunt.
- Small village misunderstandings.
- The comedy of daily routines.

Humor should never undercut sincerity. The opening hour needs warmth, not parody.

Jay Jay's awkwardness with praise is a key tonal anchor. It makes him approachable without making him foolish.

---

## 18. The First Mystery

The distant island is the first mystery.

It should appear near the end of the opening sequence, after the player has already begun to feel connected to home.

The island should be barely visible through morning mist. It should be uncertain enough that the player asks:

- Was that real?
- Has Jay Jay seen it before?
- Why did no one mention it?
- Why did he smile?

No answer should be provided in the first hour.

The mystery belongs to the player.

The island is not a threat in this moment. It is a question.

---

## 19. End State Of The First Hour

At the end of the first hour, the player should feel:

**I do not want to leave, but I have to know what is beyond the horizon.**

This is the desired emotional tension.

Leaving home should not feel like escaping boredom. It should feel like stepping away from something precious because curiosity has become too strong to ignore.

The game earns adventure by first making stillness meaningful.

---

## 20. What The Opening Must Avoid

The first hour should avoid:

- Beginning with combat.
- Beginning with catastrophe.
- Beginning with prophecy.
- Explaining the world too quickly.
- Turning the village into a checklist.
- Treating Jay Jay as a chosen hero too early.
- Using music to force grandeur before intimacy is established.
- Making the island feel like an immediate danger.
- Replacing curiosity with urgency.
- Teaching systems at the expense of emotional attachment.

The opening should trust the player to care.

---

## 21. Canon Preservation Rules

Future revisions to the opening sequence must preserve:

- The black-screen natural sound opening.
- The first visual image of pencil on paper.
- Jay Jay sketching beneath a tree overlooking the sea.
- The protected first character moment with the child and sketchbook.
- Jay Jay as observer before hero.
- The village as a living home before it is a narrative stake.
- The first hour's emphasis on curiosity over urgency.
- The distant island as an unexplained horizon mystery.
- The final emotional tension: reluctance to leave paired with desire to know.

Changes may adjust pacing, staging, route, environmental density, or supporting interactions, but they must not reverse the emotional philosophy of the opening.

---

## 22. Narrative Director Note

The first hour of Alamat ni Jay Jay is intentionally designed as the emotional opposite of many traditional adventure and JRPG openings.

The story does not begin by proving the world is in danger.

It begins by proving the world is loved.

Jay Jay's first heroic trait is not strength. It is attention. His sketchbook shows that he has already been carrying Alamat inside him before the adventure begins.

The opening hour must make players understand that when Jay Jay eventually leaves home, he is not leaving an origin point behind. He is carrying it forward.
