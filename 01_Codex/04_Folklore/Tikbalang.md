## Legend

Villagers say a Tikbalang can twist a road until a proud traveler returns to the same tree three times, each time more certain that everyone else is wrong. Guides tell children to greet lonely paths aloud, because some paths listen.

The common version says Tikbalang confuse people for amusement. Older versions say they confuse people to keep them away from places they are not ready to enter.

## History

Tikbalang stories are oldest along routes that connect villages to ancestral ruins, flood-prone crossings, and forest shrines. Some scholars believe the legends preserve practical travel knowledge: where mist gathers, where old roads split, where ruins become unstable after storms.

Local elders disagree on whether Tikbalang are ancient guardians, cursed wanderers, or simply creatures whose sense of direction is different from human sense.

## Behavior

When unthreatened, a Tikbalang observes from the edges of paths, imitating footfalls, snapping branches just behind travelers, or moving markers by a few steps. It rarely attacks first.

It is drawn to arrogance, carelessness, loud boasts, and travelers who ignore warnings. It may test a party by rearranging landmarks, separating the overconfident from the patient, or forcing travelers to admit they are lost.

It can also protect children, lost workers, or grieving travelers by making dangerous routes impossible to find.

## Habitat

Tikbalang are associated with the River Interior, mist forests, old causeways, shrine trees, abandoned watch hills, and paths where human roads cross older routes.

They favor places where visibility, memory, and direction are unreliable: foggy ridges, moonlit bamboo roads, flood-shifted riverbanks, and ruin zones where maps stop agreeing with the land.

## Relationship with Humans

Travelers fear them. Experienced guides respect them. Children laugh at stories about stubborn adults being turned around all night, but nobody laughs when someone disappears near an old boundary marker.

Some villages leave small route offerings before entering forest paths. Others treat the practice as superstition until a storm season proves them wrong.

Tikbalang encounters should allow humor without making the creature silly. The humor comes from human pride being gently punished.

## Combat Style

Tikbalang fights should feel disorienting rather than purely violent.

- Uses misdirection, forced turns, formation disruption, false openings, and status effects tied to confusion or reversed input.
- Avoids direct damage unless cornered, insulted, or guarding a taboo route.
- May leave combat if the party proves respect, solves the route logic, or stops chasing it blindly.

Combat style must come after behavior, habitat, and relationship with humans are defined.

## Weaknesses

- Patient observation.
- Respectful greetings at boundary spaces.
- Remembered landmarks, rhythmic counting, or route songs.
- Clear bells, bamboo clappers, or other sounds that establish direction.
- Refusal to boast, rush, or split the party.

## Role in Ecosystem

Tikbalang protect dangerous routes from careless traffic and indirectly preserve old groves, unstable ruins, and sacred boundaries. Their presence can indicate that a path is not merely physically dangerous, but socially or spiritually restricted.

## Visual Design

Tall, angular silhouette built for sudden half-glimpses through trees rather than full reveal. Long limbs, rain-dark hair or mane elements, weathered skin tones, and ornaments gathered from lost paths: vine knots, broken charms, old bells, river stones.

The design should emphasize height, watchfulness, and impossible posture. It should not look like a generic horse-headed monster. Its body language should shift between eerie stillness and startling speed.

## Sounds

Audio identity should begin with environmental uncertainty: a second set of footsteps, a branch cracking where nobody stands, a distant chuckle that might be bamboo, a bell heard from the wrong direction.

In battle, its sound palette should use hooflike knocks, breath in fog, low amused vocalizations, and sudden silence before a turn in the maze.
