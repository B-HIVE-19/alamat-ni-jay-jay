## Legend

People whisper that an Aswang sounds like it is far away when it is near, and near when it is far away. Some say it slips through storm noise. Some say it hides behind ordinary faces.

The legend survives because something in it is true. The danger is deciding which part.

## History

Aswang stories are carried by frightened families, isolated roads, abandoned houses, storm seasons, and political accusations. Some accounts describe genuine predatory beings. Others may preserve memories of disease, famine, betrayal, exile, or communities turning fear against outsiders.

This creature line should be handled with care: fear can reveal truth, but fear can also harm innocent people.

## Behavior

True Aswang behavior should be patient, opportunistic, and intelligent. They observe patterns, exploit isolation, and prefer confusion over open confrontation.

Rumored Aswang behavior varies wildly by region. Some details are accurate survival knowledge; others are village gossip, panic, or deliberate lies.

Encounters should often begin as investigation: missing livestock, wrong shadows, too-quiet roads, contradictory witnesses, and someone blamed too quickly.

## Habitat

Outskirts, storm roads, abandoned houses, unlit paths between villages, mangrove edges, isolated farms, and social spaces where trust has already weakened.

Aswang should feel tied to human fear as much as geography. Their best habitat is a community that no longer knows whom to believe.

## Relationship with Humans

Humans fear Aswang deeply. Communities create taboos, protective practices, accusations, jokes, and bedtime warnings around them.

Some people weaponize Aswang rumors to punish rivals or strangers. This creates story opportunities where the party must distinguish between a real threat and social cruelty.

Not every Aswang-related quest should end in killing something. Some should end in exposing a lie, protecting an accused person, or understanding why a danger appeared.

## Combat Style

Aswang battles should be tense, predatory, and psychologically uncomfortable.

- Uses ambush, draining attacks, fear, isolation, mimicry, night conditions, and retreat.
- Stronger when the party is separated, exhausted, or misled.
- Boss versions should be built around investigation payoffs: what the player learned determines weaknesses, arena conditions, or who survives.

Combat style must come after behavior, habitat, and relationship with humans are defined.

## Weaknesses

- Accurate identification of the true threat.
- Community solidarity and lighted gathering spaces.
- Protective materials, rituals, or sounds to be defined through research and original worldbuilding.
- Hunger patterns, storm timing, or territory limits.
- Exposure: some Aswang lose power when rumor becomes evidence.

## Role in Ecosystem

If treated as a true creature, an Aswang is an apex night predator or corruption-linked being that exploits weakened social and ecological systems. If treated as a rumor category, it reveals what a community fears, hides, or refuses to discuss.

Both uses can coexist, but the Codex must track which is active in any given quest or region.

## Visual Design

Visual design should avoid generic vampire, zombie, or horror-monster shorthand. The strongest direction is partial recognition: too-long stillness, wrong posture, eyes catching light at the wrong angle, storm-wet clothing, shadow distortion, and forms that imply transformation without overexplaining it.

Color palette should lean on night rain, old blood darkened by shadow, sickly lamplight, and ordinary village materials made uncanny.

## Sounds

Distance-confusing calls, roof creaks, wet footsteps, insect silence, breath near the ear, storm cover, and a voice that almost resembles someone trusted.

Music should use restraint. The player should fear the moment before certainty.
