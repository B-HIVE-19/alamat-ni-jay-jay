## Identity

Related Folklore Inspiration:
Diwata

Original Alamat Interpretation:
Local powers of place, balance, weather, growth, and obligation. In Alamat, Diwata are not generic fairies or interchangeable nature spirits; each one should be tied to a specific place, agreement, memory, or wound in the land.

Design Status:
Draft interpretation for review.

## Legend

People say a Diwata may bless a spring, guard a terrace, appear in a dream before disaster, or withdraw from a place that has forgotten gratitude.

Some communities describe Diwata as beautiful and merciful. Others speak carefully, because beauty does not mean safety and mercy does not mean permission.

## History

Diwata legends may predate current kingdoms, linking them to ancient terraces, sacred springs, reef gates, volcanic slopes, and ruin zones. Their stories often preserve old agreements: when to harvest, where not to build, what to offer, and what must never be taken twice.

Conflicting regional versions are encouraged. One Diwata may be remembered as a protector in one village and a punisher in another because both memories preserve part of the truth.

## Behavior

Diwata are attentive to imbalance, disrespect, and forgotten obligations. They may communicate through weather shifts, impossible flowers, dreams, animal behavior, reflected voices, or sudden changes in water clarity.

They do not need to appear often. Their absence can be just as meaningful as their presence.

When they act directly, it should feel ceremonial, unsettling, and emotionally precise rather than flashy.

## Habitat

Sacred springs, terrace edges, old groves, reef shrines, volcanic sanctuaries, ancestral ruins, and places where survival depends on respecting natural cycles.

A Diwata should belong to a place deeply enough that moving them would change the place's identity.

## Relationship with Humans

Humans petition, thank, fear, bargain with, misunderstand, exploit, or protect them. Some villages maintain careful practices around offerings and taboos. Others have reduced old respect into empty habit.

A Diwata can be a protector, judge, victim of neglect, ally, antagonist, or distant presence. They should never exist only to hand out magic.

## Combat Style

Direct Diwata battles should be rare and narratively serious.

- Uses terrain, weather, healing denial, binding light, growth, water, or silence depending on place identity.
- Tests whether the party understands the local imbalance.
- May end combat when the party restores respect, speaks truth, or refuses a destructive option.

Combat style must come after behavior, habitat, and relationship with humans are defined.

## Weaknesses

- Broken agreements that weaken their bond to a place.
- Pollution, desecration, forgotten rituals, or ancient seals.
- Honest confession or fulfilled obligation, depending on the conflict.
- Overextension when protecting a damaged domain.
- Human misunderstanding that can harm them indirectly.

## Role in Ecosystem

Diwata represent and regulate fragile balances: spring flow, crop rhythm, reef health, forest renewal, volcanic warning signs, or ancestral boundaries. Their condition can signal the condition of a region.

## Visual Design

Each Diwata needs a place-specific silhouette and palette. A spring Diwata should not look like a volcanic Diwata. Materials should come from the domain: water sheen, rice gold, volcanic glass, coral, moss, moonlit leaves, carved stone, woven offerings.

Avoid generic wings, sparkling fairy language, or ornamental fantasy crowns. Their beauty should feel rooted, strange, and culturally specific to Alamat.

## Sounds

Place-led audio: water over stone, distant kulintang-like resonance if culturally appropriate after research, leaf hush, low choral breath, insects stopping, terrace wind, reef water, or a voice that seems to arrive through the environment.

Their motifs should be memorable but sparing, more like a place remembering its own song than a character announcing themselves.
