## Identity

Kapre

Original Alamat Interpretation:
A solitary ancient-tree presence tied to memory, patience, and the uneasy border between village life and old forest. A Kapre in Alamat is not automatically an enemy; it is someone the village has learned to live beside, argue with, fear, and sometimes depend on.

## Legend

People say a Kapre sits where the oldest trees remember things humans would rather forget. At night, a reddish ember may appear high in the branches even when no fire is visible below.

Some legends say a Kapre curses anyone who disrespects its tree. Others say it only becomes cruel after generations of broken promises.

## History

Kapre stories cluster around shrine trees, hardwood forests, abandoned clearings, and village borders where expansion met older agreements. In some places, elders keep a record of which trees must never be cut.

There are rumors that certain Kapre remember pre-village histories and can describe buildings, families, and roads that no longer exist.

## Behavior

Kapre are slow to act but difficult to ignore. They watch routines, learn family patterns, and notice who lies about what they took from the forest.

When calm, a Kapre may be almost neighborly: dropping fruit near a hungry child, blocking a dangerous road with fallen branches, or muttering complaints about noisy festivals.

When angered, it creates oppressive stillness, smoke-heavy air, warped shadows, and the feeling that the forest itself is refusing passage.

## Habitat

Old hardwood forests, shrine trees, village edges, ancestral ruin zones reclaimed by roots, and paths where logging, farming, or roadbuilding has created tension with the land.

Kapre should be tied to specific trees or groves, not scattered randomly across maps.

## Relationship with Humans

The relationship is personal and local. One village may treat a Kapre as a terrifying taboo; another may treat it as a cranky elder who must be greeted properly.

Children dare each other to look up into forbidden branches. Adults pretend not to believe the stories while carefully avoiding certain trees after dark.

Kapre can become quest givers, obstacles, witnesses, or bosses depending on how people have treated the place it protects.

## Combat Style

Kapre combat should feel heavy, rooted, and territorial.

- Uses smoke, intimidation, crushing branch strikes, shadow reach, and terrain control.
- Punishes repeated aggression but may respond to restraint, offerings, or proof of a broken human promise.
- Boss versions should feel like fighting the consequence of a community's choices, not simply a large forest monster.

Combat style must come after behavior, habitat, and relationship with humans are defined.

## Weaknesses

- Honest negotiation.
- Restoring a damaged grove or resolving a broken promise.
- Wind, rain, or cleansing rituals that disperse smoke-heavy magic.
- Mobility limits away from its anchor tree or grove.
- Evidence that changes its understanding of a human conflict.

## Role in Ecosystem

Kapre preserve old-growth habitats by making reckless extraction costly. Their presence allows rare herbs, nesting animals, and root-bound ruins to survive. They also serve as memory keepers for land agreements that humans conveniently forget.

## Visual Design

Massive vertical silhouette half-hidden by branches, barklike skin texture, ash-gray and deep brown palette, ember accents, vine or root ornaments, and clothing fragments gathered over many decades.

Avoid generic giant design. The Kapre should feel like an old presence with personality: tired eyes, amused contempt, slow gestures, and a seated posture that still feels dangerous.

## Sounds

Low chest resonance, creaking wood, ember crackle, leaves moving without wind, slow breath, and distant muttering. Its silence should make nearby insects and birds go quiet.

Dialogue, if used, should be sparse and dry, with the rhythm of someone who has watched too many human mistakes repeat.
