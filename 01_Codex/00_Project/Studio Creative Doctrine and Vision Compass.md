## Purpose

AS-011 preserves the creative identity of *Alamat ni Jay Jay* throughout its full development lifecycle.

This is not a story document. It is not a gameplay design document. It is the creative compass that every future design decision, artwork, gameplay system, narrative sequence, environment, character, dungeon, cinematic, and production milestone should be measured against.

Its purpose is to prevent creative drift during a project that may span many years.

Future ideas should expand these doctrines rather than replace them. If a proposal conflicts with these doctrines, the conflict must be identified before implementation.

## Authority Relationship

AS-003, the Alamat Doctrine, remains the approved top-level creative doctrine and decision filter.

AS-011 functions as the studio's long-term vision compass. It translates the heart of AS-003 into practical creative review language for major proposals, reviews, milestones, and production decisions.

AS-011 does not override existing canon, Character Locks, ART-000, ART-001, ART-002, AC-series bibles, PROD-series planning documents, or the Decision Log.

## Section 1 - The Story We Are Trying To Tell

*Alamat ni Jay Jay* exists because some adventures are built from love before they are built from systems, maps, or battles.

This game is a tribute, but it is not a biography. Jay Jay is an original fictional hero inspired by the qualities Jeffrey Moralde loved most about his late brother. In this world, Jay Jay becomes the hero he always dreamed of being: not because he is the strongest, loudest, chosen, or most certain person in the room, but because he keeps choosing compassion, courage, loyalty, curiosity, and hope when the road becomes difficult.

The player should remember *Alamat ni Jay Jay* as a journey that felt warm even when it became dangerous. They should remember standing at the edge of places they did not understand and wanting to step forward. They should remember companions who felt like people, villages that felt older than the quest, dungeons that seemed to be protecting old wounds, and legends that were never completely false.

Twenty years after finishing the game, players should not only remember a plot twist or a final boss. They should remember how the adventure made them feel: the pull of the horizon, the comfort of friendship, the ache of loss, the humor that arrived when everyone needed it, the quiet pride of helping someone without expecting glory, and the feeling that this world still existed after the credits.

The emotional promise is simple:

The player should feel that they began with an ordinary village boy and ended with a legend whose greatness came from his heart.

## Section 2 - Creative Doctrines

### Doctrine 001 - Adventure Before Destination

Core Statement:
Players should always be excited about what lies beyond the horizon.

Purpose:
The journey must feel valuable even before the player knows the full destination. Curiosity, travel, uncertainty, and discovery are not filler between story moments. They are the soul of the adventure.

Design Implications:

- Routes should suggest mystery, danger, beauty, or local history.
- Major milestones should open new questions, not only close old ones.
- Optional exploration should reward attention with meaning.
- Travel should create memories through weather, music, banter, landmarks, and risk.

Examples:

- A distant ruin seen from a village road becomes a future destination.
- A quiet boat ride reveals character tension through silence rather than exposition.
- A side path contains a local shrine that changes how the player understands a village rumor.

Creative Risks:

- Treating travel as dead space.
- Moving the player from objective to objective without wonder.
- Designing locations only as stops on a checklist.

Cross References:
AS-003, AC-201, AC-200, AC-500, PROD-000.

### Doctrine 002 - Characters Before Mechanics

Core Statement:
Players remember people longer than systems.

Purpose:
Every system should strengthen emotional investment. Companions should be memorable because of who they are, not because of what slot they fill in combat.

Design Implications:

- Party members must have independent lives, fears, habits, humor, and values.
- Mechanics should express personality only after personality is established.
- Dialogue, animation, portraits, and battle behavior should reinforce character truth.
- Relationship changes should feel earned over time.

Examples:

- A combat behavior reflects a character's protective instinct rather than a generic class label.
- A campfire exchange reveals insecurity behind confidence.
- A character refuses a mechanically convenient action because it would violate who they are.

Creative Risks:

- Designing characters around party roles first.
- Making companions exist only to support Jay Jay.
- Letting mechanics contradict established personality.

Cross References:
AS-003, AC-100, AC-111, AC-112, ART-001.

### Doctrine 003 - Every Legend Has Truth

Core Statement:
Legends are incomplete rather than false.

Purpose:
The world should reward players who listen to stories, compare accounts, explore old places, and notice contradictions. Legends preserve truth imperfectly because memory changes as it travels.

Design Implications:

- Myths should point toward real history, places, beings, or consequences.
- Conflicting versions of a legend should reveal culture, fear, bias, or loss.
- Truth should be earned through exploration and interpretation.

Examples:

- A village warning exaggerates a Guardian's cruelty but preserves the location of a real danger.
- A song remembers a betrayal but misnames who caused it.
- A dungeon mural contradicts the version told by local elders.

Creative Risks:

- Using legends as decorative flavor only.
- Making myths fully false for surprise.
- Explaining every mystery too early.

Cross References:
AS-003, AC-200, AC-400, AC-430, AC-500.

### Doctrine 004 - Every Village Remembers

Core Statement:
Each community preserves its own version of history.

Purpose:
Villages should feel like living communities shaped by memory, pride, fear, grief, work, and inherited belief. NPC stories should represent what people believe, not necessarily objective truth.

Design Implications:

- Local customs should connect to history, geography, work, and legend.
- NPC dialogue should carry bias, affection, rumor, and inherited misunderstanding.
- Later discoveries may complicate what earlier villages believed.

Examples:

- Fisherfolk remember a sea spirit as a thief because their nets were destroyed, while another village remembers the same being as a protector.
- A village festival celebrates a heroic event that was partly a tragedy.
- Children repeat a simplified version of a warning adults barely understand.

Creative Risks:

- Making villages interchangeable.
- Turning NPCs into exposition devices.
- Treating local belief as either fully true or fully wrong.

Cross References:
AC-200, AC-240, AC-250, AC-201, PROD-000.

### Doctrine 005 - Every Dungeon Preserves History

Core Statement:
Dungeons are archaeological storytelling.

Purpose:
Every dungeon exists because something happened there. The player should feel they are entering a place with memory, not a combat hallway.

Design Implications:

- Dungeons require history, legend, purpose, ecology, architecture, landmarks, secrets, and emotional intent.
- Layouts should communicate what the place once was and what it became.
- Boss encounters should emerge from the dungeon's history, not appear as detached obstacles.

Examples:

- A flooded shrine shows signs of both worship and evacuation.
- A first-person corridor frames a distant sealed door as a question long before the player can open it.
- Hidden rooms reveal a more human history beneath a feared legend.

Creative Risks:

- Generic caves.
- Puzzle rooms with no cultural or historical logic.
- Dungeons that are remembered only by enemy types.

Cross References:
AS-003, AC-500, AC-510, AC-520, AC-201.

### Doctrine 006 - Guardians Are More Than Monsters

Core Statement:
Ancient beings inspired by Filipino folklore should never automatically exist as enemies.

Purpose:
Guardians, spirits, and ancient beings should feel like inhabitants of the world with their own histories and motivations. Some protect. Some guide. Some oppose. Some have fallen.

Design Implications:

- Creature design should consider ecology, memory, relationship to humans, and moral complexity.
- Not every encounter should resolve through violence.
- Folklore inspiration must be treated respectfully and transformed into original fantasy.

Examples:

- A feared Guardian protects a dangerous place because the village forgot why it was sealed.
- A spirit refuses to help because past humans broke an oath.
- A fallen being is tragic, not simply evil.

Creative Risks:

- Making folklore creatures generic enemies.
- Flattening cultural inspiration into monster lists.
- Removing mystery by overexplaining ancient beings.

Cross References:
AC-400, AC-410, AC-420, AC-430, ART-002, AC-201.

### Doctrine 007 - Truth Must Be Earned

Core Statement:
Players should never receive complete explanations through exposition alone.

Purpose:
Truth becomes meaningful when the player participates in uncovering it. Observation, exploration, environmental storytelling, conversation, and experience should carry discovery.

Design Implications:

- Use ruins, objects, music, NPC disagreement, creature behavior, and dungeon layout to reveal information.
- Let players form theories before confirming them.
- Reserve direct explanation for moments that have been properly earned.

Examples:

- A broken bridge, an old shrine, and a changed village ritual imply a past disaster before anyone names it.
- A companion notices a contradiction but does not solve it immediately.
- A boss arena reveals the cost of an old decision without a speech.

Creative Risks:

- Long exposition scenes.
- Confusing mystery with withholding basic clarity.
- Explaining emotional meaning instead of letting players feel it.

Cross References:
AC-201, AC-300, AC-500, AC-112.

### Doctrine 008 - Compassion Before Judgment

Core Statement:
Jay Jay's defining strength is compassion.

Purpose:
Jay Jay seeks understanding before violence. His humility and kindness are heroic, but compassion is not naivety. Over the course of the journey, he must learn that wisdom requires both empathy and discernment.

Design Implications:

- Jay Jay should not be written as overly confident, aggressive, or traditionally heroic by default.
- His first instinct should be to understand, but not to ignore danger.
- His growth should show the difference between mercy, avoidance, courage, and judgment.

Examples:

- Jay Jay asks why a being attacks before assuming it is evil.
- He helps someone others dismiss, but later learns kindness must include boundaries.
- A major choice asks him to protect others without losing compassion.

Creative Risks:

- Making Jay Jay passive.
- Making compassion solve every conflict easily.
- Turning him into a chosen warrior archetype detached from his humble center.

Cross References:
AC-100, PROD-001, AC-201, AS-003.

### Doctrine 009 - The World Evolves

Core Statement:
The game continually evolves its own storytelling.

Purpose:
The first villages teach one lesson. Later villages challenge that lesson. Players should not assume every Guardian is misunderstood, every village lies, or every legend resolves the same way.

Design Implications:

- Avoid repetitive moral formulas.
- Let later regions complicate assumptions established earlier.
- Make player understanding mature alongside Jay Jay's emotional growth.

Examples:

- An early Guardian is misunderstood; a later Guardian is genuinely dangerous.
- A village that seems fearful turns out to be protecting a painful truth.
- A legend that once felt comforting becomes unsettling after new evidence.

Creative Risks:

- Repeating the same twist structure.
- Making every conflict morally identical.
- Teaching the player a rule and never challenging it.

Cross References:
AC-200, AC-201, AC-300, AC-500, PROD-050.

### Doctrine 010 - Wonder Before Spectacle

Core Statement:
Quiet moments create lasting memory.

Purpose:
Discovery, friendship, landscape, music, mystery, humor, and silence can be more powerful than constant action. Spectacle should support wonder, not replace it.

Design Implications:

- Give the player room to breathe between major events.
- Let music and environment carry emotion.
- Preserve small human moments during large adventures.
- Use spectacle sparingly so it feels meaningful.

Examples:

- The party watches sunrise over an unknown island before continuing.
- A village song returns later in a changed emotional context.
- A small act of kindness becomes more memorable than a large battle.

Creative Risks:

- Escalating every scene until nothing feels intimate.
- Replacing emotional truth with visual noise.
- Treating quiet pacing as empty pacing.

Cross References:
AS-003, AC-201, ART-000, AC-800, PROD-000.

## Section 3 - Creative Review Checklist

Before approving any major feature, ask:

- Does it support the Core Design Pillars and AS-003 Alamat Doctrine?
- Does it strengthen Jay Jay's character?
- Does it reinforce the emotional journey defined in AC-201?
- Does it preserve Filipino inspiration while remaining original IP?
- Does it create wonder?
- Does it deepen exploration?
- Does it strengthen rather than weaken existing doctrine?
- Does it protect characters as people before roles?
- Does it avoid premature canon locks?
- Does it move the project closer to a memorable, playable adventure?

If the answer to any essential question is no, revise before approval.

## Section 4 - Creative Drift Protection

Common warning signs include:

- Adding mechanics before purpose.
- Creating dungeons without narrative meaning.
- Making folklore-inspired beings generic enemies.
- Overusing plot twists.
- Losing emotional pacing.
- Making Jay Jay overly confident or traditionally heroic.
- Prioritizing spectacle over wonder.
- Losing the sense of exploration.
- Confusing mystery with confusion.
- Treating villages as information kiosks instead of communities.
- Letting promotional art overwrite Character Locks.
- Treating ART-000 as final character canon instead of emotional North Star.
- Designing systems that require more content than the production plan can sustain.
- Chasing trends that weaken the identity of Alamat.
- Making the tribute foundation too literal or too invisible.

When these warning signs appear, the proposal should be reviewed against AS-003, AS-011, AC-201, PROD-000, and the relevant discipline bible before implementation continues.

## Section 5 - Studio Commitment

1910 Creative Studios is not creating a game that imitates *Phantasy Star*.

The studio is creating an original JRPG that recreates the feelings of wonder, mystery, companionship, exploration, emotional storytelling, and unforgettable adventure that inspired us to become creators.

The project's inspiration comes from the Philippines, but the world is entirely original.

Jay Jay becomes a legend not because destiny chose him, but because of the choices he makes.

The studio commitment is to protect the emotional heart of *Alamat ni Jay Jay* across every production phase: vision, documentation, prototype, vertical slice, full production, release, and whatever comes after.

## Narrative Director Notes

Preserving creative intent matters more than preserving individual ideas.

Individual ideas can change, improve, merge, or retire. The heart of the project must not. A dungeon name can change. A mechanic can change. A village layout can change. A scene can be rewritten. But the feeling of Alamat must remain: adventure, humility, wonder, memory, friendship, mystery, compassion, and hope.

AS-011 protects the project from losing itself gradually. Creative drift rarely happens in one obvious mistake. It happens when many reasonable choices slowly pull the work away from its original emotional promise. This document gives the team a way to notice that drift early and course-correct before it becomes part of the game's identity.

The purpose is not to limit creativity. It is to make sure every creative expansion still serves the same timeless vision.
