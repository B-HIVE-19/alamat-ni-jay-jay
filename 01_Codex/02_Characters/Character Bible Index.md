## Purpose

This index tracks official character documents, canon status, relationship dependencies, and open development questions.

The Character Bible exists to make every major person in *Alamat ni Jay Jay* feel like someone who would continue living a meaningful life even if Jay Jay never entered the story.

Every character should feel like someone who existed before the game began. The audience should sense that each person had routines, relationships, disappointments, private jokes, obligations, habits, and dreams before the plot reached them.

ART-004, Character Representation Doctrine, governs how closely character visuals may resemble real-life inspirations. Main-four characters become original fantasy heroes; family/supporting cast may carry stronger recognition; cameos may closely resemble real-life counterparts.

ART-005, Character Artwork Recognition Standard, governs how artists preserve recognizable identity, expression, body language, body type, costume identity, companion treatment, and family-recognition signals within the applicable ART-004 tier.

## Narrative Director Standard

Characters must feel like real people before they are treated as RPG party members, quest givers, rivals, mentors, merchants, bosses, or combat units.

Do not create characters to fill gameplay roles. Create people with inner lives, histories, contradictions, social habits, private hopes, fears, humor, personal flaws, and emotional futures. Combat roles come after personality.

Characters should be emotionally memorable before they become mechanically useful.

## Character Development Standards

Every character must be designed as a person first and a gameplay unit second. No character should exist only because the game needs a healer, tank, mage, rogue, rival, mentor, or love interest.

Each character bible should define personal history, dreams, fears, goals, humor, relationships, speech patterns, values, daily life, personal growth, combat role, appearance, animation personality, and distinct visual identity.

## Required Character Question

Each character document must answer:

> If Jay Jay never existed, what would this person be doing?

This answer should be specific, grounded, and useful for story design. It should reveal the character's independent desires, pressures, routines, and possible future.

## Before the Game Test

Each major character must answer:

> Who was this person before the player met them?

The answer should include:

- What their ordinary day looked like.
- Who depended on them.
- What they wanted before the adventure interrupted them.
- What they were avoiding.
- What private habit, responsibility, or ritual made them feel real.
- What they believed about themselves that may not be true.

## Creative Rules

1. Avoid generic RPG archetypes unless they are complicated by specific human behavior.
2. Build personal history before assigning combat role.
3. Let relationships evolve through choices, conflict, affection, disappointment, and time.
4. Give each character a distinct sense of humor, even if it is dry, awkward, gentle, theatrical, or rare.
5. Make dialogue sound natural and memorable without forcing catchphrases.
6. Track emotional growth as a series of believable changes, not a single dramatic switch.
7. Preserve approved canon unless explicitly changed by Jeffrey Moralde.
8. A character's dialogue should become recognizable even if the speaker's name is hidden.
9. Visual identity should express personality, history, region, habits, and emotional posture, not just class or combat role.
10. Every major character should have something they are wrong about.
11. Dreams and fears should be specific enough to affect choices.
12. Daily life should shape dialogue, clothing, animation, and combat behavior.
13. Animation personality should express inner life: confidence, restlessness, caution, humor, pride, warmth, grief, or fear.
14. Appearance should emerge from history, region, values, work, climate, and habit before style.

## Dialogue Recognition Test

For major characters, sample dialogue should eventually pass this test:

> If the speaker name were removed, could the player still tell who is talking?

Voice should emerge from rhythm, word choice, emotional restraint or openness, humor, silence, questions, evasions, regional texture, and what the character notices first.

Avoid relying on catchphrases as a shortcut for voice.

For full dialogue standards, see `../01_Story/Dialogue/Dialogue Direction.md`.

## Independent Life Test

Every major character should be able to exist outside Jay Jay's story.

Their document should clarify:

- What they want before meeting Jay Jay.
- Who matters to them besides Jay Jay.
- What problem they would still be dealing with if the main plot never began.
- What daily routines, responsibilities, loyalties, or private rituals make them feel lived-in.
- What future they might pursue if the adventure did not interrupt them.

## Character Bible Template

Use this structure for major characters:

| Section | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Identity | Name, status, canon state, authorship, inspiration. |
| Core Read | One-paragraph summary of who the character is as a person. |
| Before the Game | Who they were before the player met them. |
| Independent Life | What they would do if Jay Jay never existed. |
| Daily Life | Routines, work, obligations, rituals, comforts, irritations, and social habits. |
| Personality | Temperament, habits, contradictions, social behavior. |
| Values | What they protect, respect, refuse, or prioritize. |
| Strengths | Human strengths before combat strengths. |
| Personal Flaws | Flaws, blind spots, bad habits, wounds, limits, and wrong beliefs. |
| Dreams | What they hope for privately or openly. |
| Goals | What they actively want. |
| Fears | What they avoid, dread, or refuse to admit. |
| Personal History | The events and relationships that shaped them. |
| Relationships | Starting dynamics and planned evolution. |
| Humor | What they find funny and how they use humor. |
| Emotional Growth | How they change over the story. |
| Character Arc | Beginning, pressure, turning points, resolution. |
| Speech Patterns | Rhythm, vocabulary, silence, interruptions, emotional tells, and recognition test. |
| Combat Role | How personality expresses itself mechanically after the person is defined. |
| Appearance | Body language, clothing, silhouette, palette, props, region, work, history, and habits. |
| Animation Personality | Idle, walk, battle stance, reactions, gestures, and movement tells. |
| Open Questions | Unresolved decisions that need creative approval. |
