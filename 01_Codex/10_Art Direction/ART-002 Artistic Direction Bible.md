## ID Assignment Note

The request named this document ART-001. ART-001 is already assigned to the Main Character Production Bible. To preserve existing project history and avoid overwriting an active production document, this Artistic Direction Bible is assigned ART-002.

## Purpose

ART-002 establishes the visual language that governs every artistic decision made throughout the development of *Alamat ni Jay Jay*.

This document does not contain concept art. It defines the artistic philosophy and visual rules that every artist, animator, UI designer, environment artist, technical artist, and cinematic artist should follow.

The goal is visual consistency across the entire project.

Every future artistic asset should be evaluated against this document.

ART-002 now works alongside ART-003, ART-004, and ART-005. ART-003 defines the protected visual soul and memory doctrine for Alamat; ART-004 defines the protected character representation doctrine; ART-005 defines character artwork recognition standards; ART-002 defines the practical execution standards artists use to realize those doctrines.

## 1. Artistic Vision

The art direction of *Alamat ni Jay Jay* should make players feel they are looking into a world that is ancient, alive, welcoming, mysterious, and worth protecting.

The visual experience should consistently evoke:

- Adventure.
- Wonder.
- Warmth.
- Curiosity.
- Friendship.
- Hope.
- Humility.
- Discovery.
- Reverence for old places.
- The feeling that every path may lead to something meaningful.

The project's visual philosophy follows:

- Adventure First.
- Characters Before Mechanics.
- Wonder Before Spectacle.

Beauty in Alamat should not exist only to impress the viewer. It should invite the player forward. A village should make the player want to meet its people. A ruin should make the player wonder who built it. A road should make the player ask where it goes. A character should feel remembered before they feel powerful.

Avoid art that feels empty, over-rendered, generic, cynical, grim by default, or designed only for spectacle.

## 2. Color Philosophy

Color in Alamat should preserve hope even during danger. The world may become frightening, stormy, lonely, or sad, but it should not become unnecessarily grim or desaturated.

### Time of Day

| Condition | Color Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Morning | Soft gold, misted greens, pale blue, warm roofs, damp earth, new-start energy. |
| Midday | Clear tropical light, strong readable colors, deep greens, bright sky, sun-warmed materials. |
| Golden Hour | Warm amber, long shadows, glowing skin tones, emotional nostalgia, adventure invitation. |
| Sunset | Rich oranges, rose clouds, blue-violet distance, reflective water, transition and memory. |
| Night | Deep blues, lantern warmth, quiet silhouettes, visible stars, mystery without total darkness. |
| Moonlight | Silver-blue highlights, soft edges, sacred calm, gentle contrast. |

### Weather and Atmosphere

| Condition | Color Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Rain | Saturated greens, darkened wood, cool blue-gray air, warm interior lights, reflective paths. |
| Storms | High contrast, green-gray skies, sharp lightning accents, danger with visible hope. |
| Fog | Muted distance, softened forms, pale greens and blues, mystery through layering. |

### World States

| Subject | Color Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Magic | Culturally grounded, restrained, meaningful; avoid generic neon glow. |
| Ancient Ruins | Moss, old stone, mineral stains, faded pigments, ritual accents, age and memory. |
| Sacred Places | Controlled color, intentional contrast, candle warmth, mineral pigments, quiet authority. |
| Villages | Local materials, food color, cloth, painted signs, weathering, human warmth. |
| Dungeons | Strong identity palettes tied to history, ecology, construction, and emotional purpose. |

Color should guide emotion and readability. It should never flatten all places into the same tropical palette.

## 3. Lighting Philosophy

Lighting tells stories before characters speak.

Lighting should reinforce emotion rather than simply realism.

| Lighting Type | Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Natural lighting | Reflect climate, time, terrain, and mood. Sunlight should make the world feel alive. |
| Interior lighting | Reveal daily life, material texture, social warmth, and class differences. |
| Lanterns | Human presence, safety, travel, memory, and fragile hope. |
| Temple lighting | Intentional, ritualized, quiet, sacred, never generic spooky. |
| Firelight | Camp intimacy, fear balanced by friendship, shifting expressions. |
| Ocean reflections | Movement, distance, longing, travel, and possibility. |
| Volumetric lighting | Use for wonder, sacredness, weather, or major reveals; avoid constant overuse. |
| Silhouettes | Clarify identity, emotion, danger, and scale. |
| Backlighting | Create mythic presence only when story meaning supports it. |
| God rays | Reserve for discovery, hope, sacred places, and awe. |
| Atmospheric haze | Show distance, humidity, storm pressure, volcanic ash, or memory. |

Lighting should never make important gameplay or emotional information unreadable.

## 4. Character Shape Language

Every major character should be recognizable by silhouette alone.

| Character / Group | Shape Language |
| --- | --- |
| Jay Jay | Open, youthful, approachable, humble, ordinary-before-legend. Avoid over-heroic shapes. |
| Kalika | Composed, observant, elegant, precise, scholarly without royal stiffness. |
| Kuya Ric | Broad, grounded, protective, courageous, warm strength rather than brute force. |
| Mark | Energetic, forward-leaning, curious, asymmetrical, lively without clutter. |
| NPCs | Region, labor, age, class, and daily life should shape silhouettes. |
| Villagers | Practical, weather-aware, tool-using, grounded in work and community. |
| Scholars | Carried knowledge, books, scrolls, satchels, careful posture, travel wear. |
| Explorers | Layered gear, route tools, weather protection, adaptable silhouettes. |
| Children | Clear age read, playfulness, local clothing, never mascot exaggeration. |
| Enemies | Readable threat, ecology, behavior, and origin before combat function. |
| Bosses | Iconic silhouette tied to story, dungeon, legend, or emotional truth. |

Silhouette clarity matters at full illustration scale, sprite scale, battle scale, and UI portrait scale.

## 5. Costume Philosophy

Costumes should feel practical, lived in, culturally inspired, and original to Alamat.

Costume inspiration may draw from:

- Filipino textiles.
- Weaving.
- Embroidery.
- Natural materials.
- Regional climate logic.
- Island travel.
- Craft traditions.
- Ceremonial restraint.
- Daily work.

Costumes must maintain originality. Avoid direct historical recreation. Avoid treating cultural inspiration as surface decoration.

Costume rules:

1. Clothing should reveal where a person lives, what they do, what they value, and how they move.
2. Materials should respond to heat, rain, boats, cliffs, forests, villages, markets, and ruins.
3. Embroidery and motifs should be meaningful, restrained, and readable.
4. Do not over-design costumes with excessive belts, metal, glowing pieces, or unrelated ornaments.
5. Travel wear should show repair, weathering, comfort, and use.
6. Character costume must follow approved Character Locks when available.

## 6. Environment Philosophy

Every location should feel like it has history.

Every environment should answer:

- Who built this?
- Who lived here?
- Why was it abandoned?
- Why does it still matter?

Avoid empty environments.

Environmental storytelling should always be present through:

- Repairs.
- Worn thresholds.
- Old foundations.
- Offerings.
- Tools left in place.
- Weathering.
- Plant growth.
- Water marks.
- Smoke stains.
- Carved posts.
- Market clutter.
- Broken paths.
- Reused ancient stones.

An environment should never feel like a stage set waiting for the player. It should feel like life was happening before Jay Jay arrived.

## 7. Architecture Philosophy

Architecture in Alamat should blend Filipino inspiration with original fantasy logic.

| Location Type | Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Villages | Climate-aware, lived-in, raised floors, wide eaves, repair marks, food prep, social spaces. |
| Cities | Layered districts, trade pressure, old foundations, civic plazas, markets, route control. |
| Temples | Sacred geometry, controlled light, ritual thresholds, local material identity. |
| Ruins | Ancient engineering, weathering, mystery, partial understanding, visible original purpose. |
| Fortresses | Terrain-aware defense, reused stone, watch paths, storm logic, political memory. |
| Markets | Awnings, baskets, food smoke, signs, gossip spaces, color variation, crowd flow. |
| Ports | Docks, boats, ropes, salt wear, warehouses, tide markers, trade authority. |
| Mountain settlements | Terraces, steep paths, mist, retaining walls, granaries, weather protection. |
| Forest settlements | Wood, woven panels, shade, shrine clearings, root-aware paths, humidity. |
| Sacred sites | Quiet composition, intentional offerings, old trees or stones, restraint. |

Architecture should reveal climate, economy, belief, status, danger, and history.

## 8. Camera Philosophy

Camera language should support emotion, readability, and story meaning.

Preferred cinematic language:

- Wide establishing shots to make the world feel explorable.
- Character close-ups for emotional truth, not vanity.
- Environmental reveals that reward curiosity.
- First-person dungeon framing that supports tension, orientation, and discovery.
- Dialogue framing that respects relationships and body language.
- Emotional moments with restraint and space.
- Boss introductions that reveal identity, scale, and story purpose.

Never use dramatic camera movement unless it serves the story.

Avoid:

- Constant camera motion.
- Overly heroic low angles for humble characters.
- Visual noise during emotional moments.
- Hiding important environmental information for style.
- Boss framing that sells spectacle before meaning.

## 9. Animation Philosophy

Animation should communicate personality before spectacle.

Animation principles:

| Animation Type | Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Idle animations | Reveal temperament, fatigue, curiosity, caution, confidence, or care. |
| Walking | Show background, mood, terrain, and relationship to companions. |
| Running | Communicate urgency without losing character identity. |
| Turning | Support awareness, fear, curiosity, and first-person spatial clarity. |
| Looking around | Make discovery and danger feel alive. |
| Celebration | Earned, character-specific, never generic victory posing. |
| Embarrassment | Natural, subtle, and human. |
| Curiosity | Leaning, pausing, glancing, touching objects carefully, asking with posture. |
| Fear | Honest and readable; bravery does not erase fear. |
| Laughter | Should come from personality and trust. |

Small gestures matter. A hand checking a satchel, a glance back at a friend, a nervous adjustment, or a quiet exhale can carry more emotion than a large flourish.

## 10. User Interface Philosophy

The UI should feel handcrafted and native to the world.

Possible material inspirations:

- Carved narra wood.
- Capiz shell.
- Brass.
- Filipino geometric motifs.
- Traditional weaving.
- Natural parchment.
- Subtle ornamentation.

UI rules:

1. Readability comes first.
2. Ornament supports function.
3. Motifs must be restrained.
4. UI should feel like it belongs to Alamat, not a generic fantasy template.
5. UI should disappear into the world rather than compete with it.
6. Combat UI should be clear under pressure.
7. Dungeon UI should support uncertainty, mapping, danger, and discovery.
8. Menu materials should feel handcrafted but not heavy.

Avoid generic fantasy frames, excessive gold trim, over-textured panels, unreadable decorative fonts, and constant glow.

## 11. Visual Consistency Rules

Every future artist must follow these rules:

1. Protect the established North Star artwork.
2. ART-000 defines emotional composition.
3. ART-002 defines artistic execution.
4. ART-001 governs main character production requirements.
5. ART-004 and ART-005 govern character representation tiers and recognition execution.
5. Do not over-design costumes.
6. Do not overuse glowing effects.
7. Prioritize readability.
8. Prioritize silhouette.
9. Prioritize emotional storytelling.
10. Preserve hope even in darkness.
11. Use Filipino inspiration respectfully and transform it into original fantasy.
12. Avoid direct historical recreation.
13. Avoid generic fantasy art.
14. Let materials, weather, and wear tell history.
15. Make characters feel like people before roles.
16. Make environments feel inhabited before the plot arrives.
17. Make bosses meaningful before they are impressive.
18. Never let spectacle overwhelm wonder.

## Narrative Director Notes

Visual consistency is essential for creating a timeless JRPG because players remember worlds as emotional places, not as isolated assets.

If every artist interprets Alamat differently, the world will feel assembled rather than discovered. Consistent art direction allows players to believe that villages, ruins, characters, UI, dungeons, creatures, and cinematics all belong to one living world.

The artistic direction should support the emotional journey rather than simply create beautiful images. Beauty matters, but beauty without intention becomes decoration. In Alamat, every color, silhouette, material, camera move, animation, and UI frame should help the player feel adventure, friendship, wonder, humility, curiosity, and hope.

The goal is not to overwhelm players with visual spectacle. The goal is to make them want to step into the world and keep going.
