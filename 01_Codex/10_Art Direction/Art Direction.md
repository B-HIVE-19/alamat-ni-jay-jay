## Directional Intent

The visual identity should make *Alamat ni Jay Jay* feel timeless, readable, and culturally specific without becoming generic fantasy.

Everything should feel unmistakably Filipino while remaining original to Alamat. The game should not directly recreate historical Philippines or rely on surface-level symbols. Visual design should transform Filipino-inspired geography, architecture, textiles, craft, folklore, island life, climate, values, and material culture into a cohesive fantasy world.

Every visual decision should reinforce the identity of Alamat.

ART-003, The Alamat Visual Bible, is the protected visual identity and memory doctrine for this art direction. AC-700 provides the broader art bible structure; ART-003 protects the emotional standard; ART-002 translates that standard into execution rules.

## Art Director Mandate

Protect visual consistency across characters, villages, dungeons, creatures, UI, props, equipment, animation, lighting, and world materials.

Never create generic fantasy art.

Every visual decision should answer:

- Does this feel like it belongs in Alamat?
- Does this reinforce the world, history, culture, climate, or character?
- Would this still be recognizable without labels?
- Is the Filipino inspiration specific, respectful, and transformed into original fantasy?
- Does this support wonder, adventure, and emotional authenticity?

## Visual Pillars

1. Filipino-inspired, never stereotypical.
2. Original fantasy, never historical recreation.
3. Readable silhouettes.
4. Climate-aware materials and clothing.
5. World history visible in surfaces, objects, ruins, and architecture.
6. Character personality visible before combat role.
7. Creatures rooted in ecology, folklore, and local belief.
8. Color palettes tied to place, emotion, faction, and story function.
9. Dungeons visually tell their own history.
10. Every major location has a distinct visual memory.

## Character Design Rules

Every major character should eventually have an official character art packet:

| Required Asset | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Official Concept Art | Canon visual reference for the character. |
| Color Palette | Approved colors, accents, material notes, and emotional read. |
| Clothing Guide | Layering, fabrics, accessories, weather logic, cultural references, and practical use. |
| Facial Expressions | Emotional range, default expression, vulnerability, anger, humor, fear, focus. |
| Signature Pose | A pose that expresses personality before combat function. |
| Animation References | Walk, idle, battle stance, reaction, gesture habits, emotional tells. |
| Height Comparison | Scale against Jay Jay, Kalika, party members, NPCs, and creatures where relevant. |
| Silhouette Recognition | Readability test from black shape alone. |

Character art must communicate history, personality, region, habits, emotional posture, and relationship to the world. Combat role comes after personhood.

Character design rules:

1. Personality must be readable before combat role.
2. Clothing should reflect climate, daily life, region, movement needs, values, and personal history.
3. Silhouette should remain recognizable in black shape alone.
4. Palettes should support identity, emotional tone, party readability, and world logic.
5. Accessories should reveal habit, memory, duty, humor, craft, or relationship.
6. Faces should support a wide emotional range, including humor, worry, anger, tenderness, fear, and resolve.
7. Designs inspired by real people must be transformed into original fictional characters.

## Architecture

Architecture should reveal how people live with rain, heat, storms, floods, spirits, trade, terrain, and memory.

Architectural rules:

- Use climate-aware forms: raised floors, steep roofs, wide eaves, ventilation, shade, storm shutters, and flood-aware walkways.
- Make materials local: bamboo, hardwood, shell, clay, stone, woven panels, rope, thatch, metal, and reused ancient blocks where appropriate.
- Show history through repairs, extensions, worn thresholds, shrine marks, carved posts, old foundations, and mismatched eras.
- Let architecture guide gameplay through alleys, stairs, bridges, courtyards, docks, rooflines, gates, thresholds, and hidden understructures.
- Keep sacred, domestic, civic, military, dungeon, and market spaces visually distinct.

## Village Styles

Villages should be visually defined by survival logic, economy, belief, and weather.

| Village Type | Visual Identity |
| --- | --- |
| Stilt Fishing Village | Raised walkways, nets, salt racks, boats, shell ornaments, storm shutters, wet wood. |
| River Crossing Village | Bridges, ferry posts, water markers, repair sheds, shrine bends, muddy paths. |
| Terrace Village | Layered fields, stone retaining walls, granaries, mist, woven storage, footpaths. |
| Shrine Village | Threshold markers, offering tables, quiet paths, carved guardians, ritual colors. |
| Market Village | Layered awnings, baskets, cloth, signage, food smoke, crowded pathways, gossip spaces. |

Every village should include evidence of daily life before Jay Jay arrives.

## Dungeon Styles

Dungeons should visually tell their own history.

Dungeon visual rules:

- Construction must show original purpose and later damage or occupation.
- Lighting should guide fear, curiosity, discovery, and relief.
- Landmarks should support first-person orientation.
- Puzzle elements should look native to the dungeon's culture, architecture, ecology, or legend.
- Boss arenas should express the dungeon's identity.
- Secret areas should feel hidden for a reason, not placed only for reward.

## Creature Design Philosophy

Creatures should be designed as living parts of the world, not generic enemies.

Creature rules:

- Root each major creature in folklore, ecology, local belief, or regional history.
- Define habitat, movement, scale, silhouette, and behavior before combat mechanics.
- Avoid simply attaching Filipino names to generic monster forms.
- Let creature design show whether locals fear, respect, joke about, avoid, hunt, feed, worship, or misunderstand it.
- Boss creatures should carry story meaning.

Every major creature should have:

- Folklore inspiration notes.
- Original Alamat interpretation.
- Silhouette study.
- Color palette.
- Habitat and ecology notes.
- Movement and animation references.
- Face or expression logic, if applicable.
- Scale comparison.
- Relationship to local belief, fear, humor, or taboo.

## Weapons

Weapons should feel crafted, inherited, traded, repaired, blessed, improvised, or discovered. They should never feel like generic loot.

Weapon design rules:

- Shape language should support character identity and region.
- Materials should reveal origin and status.
- Upgrades should be visible when possible.
- Ceremonial, practical, ancestral, and dungeon-found weapons should look different.
- Combat readability matters, but personality and world logic come first.

## Armor

Armor should reflect climate, movement, materials, local danger, and character identity.

Armor design rules:

- Avoid generic metal armor as the default.
- Use layered cloth, leather, woven reinforcement, wood, shell, metal plates, charms, padding, and regional craft where appropriate.
- Armor should allow animation and readable silhouettes.
- Protective gear should show what the wearer expects to survive: rain, blades, claws, spirits, ash, thorns, or dungeons.

## Materials

Materials should communicate region, craft, climate, economy, and age.

Core material families:

- Bamboo, hardwood, rattan, rope, woven fibers, clay, shell, stone, metal, volcanic glass, water-worn wood, moss, ash, cloth, leather, bone, paper, and old ruin stone.

Material rules:

- Materials should weather believably.
- Expensive materials should reveal trade, status, rarity, or ritual function.
- Ancient materials should look distinct from current settlement craft.
- Equipment materials should reveal origin and upgrade history.

## Textures

Textures should feel tactile and specific: woven, carved, salt-worn, rain-darkened, smoke-stained, moss-covered, sun-bleached, ash-dusted, polished by hands, cracked by roots, or repaired over generations.

Texture rules:

- Use wear to tell history.
- Avoid clean fantasy surfaces unless the location has a reason.
- Make repeated textures vary by region and craft tradition.
- Let close-up textures support inspection, secrets, and dungeon storytelling.

## Color Theory

Color should support readability, emotional tone, and regional identity. Avoid one-note palettes. Major regions, dungeons, and factions should have distinguishable color logic without becoming artificial.

Color theory rules:

- Assign palette logic by character, region, faction, dungeon, and emotional function.
- Use accent colors deliberately for memory, danger, magic, loyalty, sacred objects, or hidden truth.
- Keep party members readable in battle and exploration.
- Avoid trend-driven palettes that weaken Alamat's identity.
- Let natural materials and climate influence saturation, contrast, and weathering.

## Lighting

Lighting should reinforce climate and story: humid glare, storm-dark interiors, lantern warmth, misted highlands, underwater green, volcanic ash haze, shrine candlelight, and old ruin shadows.

Lighting rules:

- Lighting should guide attention without feeling artificial.
- Dungeon lighting should support orientation, fear, mystery, and discovery.
- Village lighting should reveal daily rhythm: morning labor, market heat, dusk gatherings, storm preparation.
- Sacred lighting should feel intentional and ritualized.
- Battle lighting should preserve readability.

## Mood

Mood should balance wonder, danger, warmth, humor, melancholy, and discovery.

Mood should come from composition, color, lighting, texture, animation, sound assumptions, weather, character posture, and environmental history. Avoid making the game visually grim by default; darkness matters more when warmth and beauty exist beside it.

## Animation Style

Animation should reveal personality, weight, fear, humor, confidence, fatigue, and relationship.

Animation rules:

- Idle animations should show character temperament.
- Walk cycles should express background and emotional posture.
- Battle stances should grow from personality before role.
- Reactions should be readable and distinct.
- Humor can appear through timing, posture, hesitation, and small gestures.
- Dungeon movement should support tension and curiosity.

## UI Style

UI should feel connected to the world without reducing Filipino inspiration to decoration. Motifs should come from approved visual systems: materials, patterns, carvings, maps, textiles, shrine markers, or navigation tools.

UI style rules:

- Prioritize readability, speed, and comfort.
- Use world-connected materials and motifs sparingly.
- Menus should feel elegant and classic without becoming ornate.
- Combat UI should be clear under pressure.
- Dungeon UI should support uncertainty, mapping, resources, danger, and discovery.
- UI should not fight the emotional tone of a scene.

## Typography

Typography should be readable first and atmospheric second.

Typography rules:

- Body text must be legible for long play sessions.
- Display type may carry Alamat identity but should not reduce Filipino inspiration to decorative imitation.
- Distinguish system text, dialogue, item names, location names, and ancient inscriptions.
- Ancient or ritual text treatments should feel tied to world history and language rules.
- Avoid novelty fonts that age poorly or harm accessibility.

## Visual Approval Tests

Before major visual approval, ask:

1. Does this avoid generic fantasy?
2. Does this feel original to Alamat?
3. Is the Filipino inspiration specific and transformed rather than pasted on?
4. Can the subject be recognized by silhouette, palette, or material language?
5. Does the design support character, place, history, ecology, or story?
6. Would players remember this image after finishing the game?
7. Does this reinforce the identity of Alamat?
