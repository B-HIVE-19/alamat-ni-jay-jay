## World Mandate

The world of *Alamat ni Jay Jay* should feel inhabited before the story begins and capable of continuing after the story ends.

This is not the historical Philippines. It is an original fantasy world inspired by Filipino folklore, Philippine geography, architecture, history, island life, ancient civilizations, and oral tradition.

The world should feel interconnected. Geography should shape trade. Trade should shape politics. Politics should shape roads, ports, conflicts, taxes, festivals, and rumors. Myths should influence how people build homes, bury the dead, name storms, avoid certain forests, and explain old ruins.

The world should appear to have existed for thousands of years before Jay Jay's journey. The player should feel that every route, village, law, shrine, market, ruin, taboo, song, and tool came from a long chain of decisions, disasters, beliefs, and adaptations.

## World Rules

1. The world existed for thousands of years before Jay Jay's adventure.
2. No region exists only as a level theme; every place needs economy, history, beliefs, and daily life.
3. Folklore is part of the world, but people disagree about what is true, sacred, dangerous, exaggerated, or misunderstood.
4. Settlements should be shaped by water, terrain, food sources, trade routes, defense needs, and spiritual taboos.
5. Ancient ruins should reveal civilizations with their own logic, not just puzzle rooms.
6. Political systems should be local, layered, and personal: councils, elders, families, guilds, shrine keepers, port authorities, and regional rulers may all overlap.
7. Languages and naming should suggest cultural depth without becoming a direct copy of real-world history.
8. Never create generic fantasy locations. Every major place must have a reason for existing, a history before Jay Jay, and a living relationship to nearby people.
9. Every legend must have purpose: warning, memory, law, grief, propaganda, ritual, navigation, social control, or hidden truth.

## Location Approval Test

Before a major location is approved, answer:

| Question | Required Standard |
| --- | --- |
| Why is this place here? | Geography, resources, defense, worship, trade, exile, ruin access, or ancestral duty should explain it. |
| What happened here before Jay Jay? | The location should have at least one historical layer older than the current story. |
| Who depends on this place? | People, creatures, spirits, guilds, families, rulers, or travelers should be affected by it. |
| What does the place believe about itself? | Local myths, pride, shame, taboos, or origin stories should shape behavior. |
| What would change if it vanished? | Trade, politics, ecology, faith, travel, or memory should be disrupted. |
| What is memorable without combat? | Architecture, ritual, weather, sound, history, layout, or human detail should carry identity. |

## Foundation Scope

AC-200 establishes the world foundation for *Alamat ni Jay Jay*. It is not a story outline and should not define plot events, character destinations, final dungeon order, final villain history, or ending revelations.

At this stage, the World Bible may approve:

- World logic.
- Cultural design principles.
- Environmental categories.
- Social systems.
- Everyday life assumptions.
- Ancient-history design rules.
- Naming and documentation standards.

At this stage, the World Bible should avoid locking:

- The official name of the world.
- The complete world map.
- Exact continent count.
- Exact nation borders.
- Final capital cities.
- Final religious hierarchy.
- Exact magical rules.
- Secret history reveals.
- Any story-critical ancient truth.

## Cultural Inspiration Guardrails

The world may be inspired by Filipino cultures, histories, languages, landscapes, architecture, folklore, and values, but it must remain original intellectual property.

Use inspiration through transformation:

- Island geography, storms, reefs, mountains, rivers, and volcanic landscapes should influence how people travel and build.
- Architecture may draw from raised homes, wide eaves, ventilation, woven panels, hardwood, bamboo, thatch, shell, stone, rope, and flood-aware construction.
- Social life may draw from family obligation, hospitality, elders, community reputation, market life, boat culture, and oral storytelling.
- Folklore inspiration should be handled through original interpretations, local disagreement, ecological context, and emotional truth.
- Food, clothing, music, and ritual should feel lived-in and practical rather than decorative.

Avoid:

- Direct historical recreation.
- Treating real Filipino cultures as interchangeable.
- Using sacred, regional, or ethnic details as surface decoration.
- Turning folklore beings into generic monsters with Filipino names.
- Locking real-world political or religious analogues into the setting.

## Geography

The primary world structure is an island realm: a chain of inhabited islands, reef roads, mountain interiors, river villages, volcanic highlands, mangrove coasts, storm channels, and ancient ruin sites.

### Core Geographic Logic

- Coasts connect people through boats, fishing, markets, and gossip.
- Mountains preserve older beliefs, hidden settlements, and dangerous paths.
- Rivers act as roads into the interior.
- Volcanic soil creates fertile settlements and feared sacred zones.
- Mangroves protect coastlines but hide smugglers, spirits, and forgotten passages.
- Reefs can be both defensive walls and deadly traps.

## Continents and Wider World

No official continent map is approved yet.

The playable foundation currently favors an archipelago or island realm rather than a continent-spanning fantasy map. This supports boat travel, island discovery, reef danger, trade routes, varied local identities, and the emotional experience of classic JRPG exploration.

Continental-scale lands may exist beyond the known island realm, but they should remain flexible until the World Bible requires them. If introduced later, distant lands should affect the setting through trade goods, old migration stories, ship designs, scholarly disputes, foreign coins, or mythic geography rather than immediate exposition.

Design rule: the world should feel bigger than the player's first map, but not so overdefined that future stories lose mystery.

## Settlements

Settlements should be built around survival first: access to water, food, shelter, trade, defense, and spiritual safety.

### Settlement Types

- Fishing villages on stilts near mangroves or reefs.
- River barangay-like communities organized around boats, bridges, and harvest cycles.
- Mountain hamlets near terraces, caves, and old watch paths.
- Port towns governed by merchant houses, harbor rules, and shrine calendars.
- Walled hill cities built around ancient foundations.
- Shrine settlements that exist because people believe a place must be tended.

## Cultures

Culture should be specific to place. Food, clothing, architecture, festivals, etiquette, taboos, and humor should vary by island, class, trade, and local history.

### Cultural Design Pillars

- Oral history matters; songs, riddles, and family stories preserve truth unevenly.
- Hospitality is powerful but not simple; accepting shelter can create obligation.
- Elders may hold authority, but younger people may control trade, navigation, or new tools.
- Ritual and practical life often overlap.
- Community reputation can matter as much as law.

## Food

Food should make the world feel warm, regional, practical, and alive. It should also reveal climate, trade, class, family, travel, and memory.

Food design principles:

- Coastal areas rely on fish, shellfish, seaweed, salt, rice trade, fermented or dried preservation, and boat-friendly meals.
- River and terrace regions rely on rice, root crops, vegetables, freshwater fish, herbs, and harvest rituals.
- Mountain settlements use preserved foods, smoked goods, hardy crops, medicinal plants, and festival foods tied to weather and altitude.
- Market towns blend regional ingredients through trade, migration, and port gossip.
- Travel food should be practical: wrapped, dried, smoked, packed in leaves, stored in jars, or shared from communal pots.

Food should appear in banter, markets, inns, festivals, offerings, family scenes, camps, and dungeon preparation. Avoid treating food only as item names.

## Clothing

Clothing should be climate-aware, regionally grounded, and character-revealing. It should never feel like generic fantasy costume.

Clothing design principles:

- Heat, rain, humidity, mud, boats, climbing, and forest travel shape what people wear.
- Layers should be light, repairable, breathable, and easy to move in unless status or ritual requires otherwise.
- Woven fibers, cloth wraps, practical belts, weather covers, sandals, travel bags, hats, and work gloves may appear where appropriate.
- Ceremonial clothing should differ from daily clothing through color, fabric care, accessories, and movement restrictions.
- Wealth should show through dye quality, clean edges, metal fasteners, fine weaving, imported cloth, or preserved heirlooms rather than generic jewels.

Party clothing must follow ART-001 and the Character Locks once approved.

## Music in Daily Life

Music should be tied to memory, labor, ritual, travel, and place.

World music may include:

- Work songs timed to rowing, planting, weaving, pounding grain, or hauling nets.
- Market calls, children's rhymes, and teasing songs.
- Shrine chants, wake songs, festival processions, and rain prayers.
- Boat songs used to keep rhythm or remember routes.
- Old melodies that preserve fragments of ancient language or lost history.

Music should not exist only as background score. People in the world should sing, hum, drum, argue over songs, misremember lyrics, and attach songs to locations.

See `../09_Music/Music Direction.md`.

## Daily Life

Daily life should be visible before the player learns a location's dramatic function.

Common daily-life details:

- Drying fish.
- Repairing nets.
- Carving boats.
- Weaving mats.
- Carrying water.
- Cooking for groups.
- Tending shrines.
- Copying route marks.
- Fixing roofs before storms.
- Teaching children stories and practical warnings.
- Counting trade goods.
- Watching river height or tide stones.
- Avoiding taboo paths at certain times.

Daily life should include humor, impatience, work, gossip, pride, fear, tenderness, and ordinary errands.

## Ancient Civilizations

Ancient civilizations should have beliefs, engineering, resource systems, architecture, language, political structures, and reasons for decline or disappearance. Ruins should be evidence of old lives, not decorative dungeon shells.

See `Ancient Ruins.md`.

### Ancient Civilization Design Rules

Ancient civilizations should never exist only to provide dungeons.

Each ancient culture must define:

- What environment it mastered.
- What resource or belief sustained it.
- How it built.
- What it feared.
- What language or symbol system it left behind.
- What modern people misunderstand about it.
- Why its ruins are still politically, spiritually, or economically important.
- What truths are preserved through myth rather than written record.

Ancient technology, if present, should feel rare, weighty, and culturally dangerous. It should create questions instead of solving all problems.

## Languages

Language should reinforce identity and history. Different regions may share trade speech while preserving local words for spirits, kinship, weather, rituals, and taboo places.

### Language Principles

- Names should feel pronounceable and culturally coherent.
- Place names may preserve old meanings that current residents only partly understand.
- Formal speech, market speech, family speech, and ritual speech can differ.
- Some old inscriptions may be readable only through songs, patterns, or inherited phrases.

See `Languages.md`.

## Daily Life

Daily life should be visible everywhere: drying fish, repairing nets, carving boats, tending shrines, pounding grain, copying maps, weaving mats, debating prices, avoiding cursed paths, and preparing for storms.

## Politics

Political power should be distributed across overlapping authorities:

- Village councils and elders.
- Port officials and toll collectors.
- Merchant families and boat guilds.
- Shrine keepers and ritual specialists.
- Regional rulers or protectors.
- Navigators who control safe routes.
- Families with old land or ruin claims.

## Nations

See `Political Systems.md` and `Nations.md`.

Political entities should not feel like generic fantasy kingdoms. They should grow from island geography, maritime routes, river basins, shrine legitimacy, ancient ruin claims, family alliances, and control of seasonal resources.

## Architecture

See `Architecture.md`.

Architecture should reveal climate, materials, wealth, risk, faith, and history. Stilt foundations, raised floors, steep roofs, shutters, courtyards, bamboo, hardwood, stone terraces, rope bridges, carved posts, reef walls, and ruin foundations should all communicate how people live with rain, heat, storms, spirits, terrain, and memory.

## Religion and Myths

Belief should feel lived-in. People may leave offerings because they believe, because their parents did, because it is polite, because it is practical, or because they are afraid not to.

Myths should contradict each other by region. The same spirit, ruin, or hero may be remembered as protector in one island and disaster in another.

Every legend should do work in the world. A legend may protect a dangerous path, preserve memory of an ancient disaster, justify political power, warn children away from ruins, encode navigation knowledge, explain a taboo, or hide a truth that powerful people want forgotten.

- `Religions and Myths.md`

## Legends and Folklore

Legends should be part of social life, not detached lore entries.

Legend functions:

- Preserve memory of real danger.
- Explain ruins, storms, disappearances, strange animals, forbidden paths, or family duties.
- Encode navigation, seasonal warnings, or ecological knowledge.
- Protect sacred or dangerous places.
- Justify leadership, land claims, debts, taboos, or rituals.
- Hide truths that people are not ready or allowed to speak plainly.

Folklore beings should be treated as part of the world. Some may be dangerous, some misunderstood, some sacred, some ordinary to locals, and some known only through conflicting stories.

See `../04_Folklore/Folklore Bible.md`, `../04_Folklore/Creature Bible.md`, and `../04_Folklore/Mythology.md`.

## Trade and Economy

Trade should connect the world materially and narratively.

WRLD-002 establishes the House of Lexi as the protected merchant hospitality standard. Commerce should not be purely transactional; shops and merchant spaces can carry welcome, trust, story exchange, practical care, rumor, humor, and memory.

### Major Trade Goods

- Rice, root crops, fruit, salt, dried fish, pearls, woven cloth, hardwood, bamboo, medicinal plants, metal ore, volcanic glass, ceramics, ritual goods, maps, and boat parts.

### Economic Pressure Points

- Storm seasons interrupt routes.
- Reefs create dangerous chokepoints.
- Mountain passes can be controlled by local powers.
- Ruins attract scholars, treasure hunters, pilgrims, and thieves.
- Spirits and taboos may make some resources politically dangerous to harvest.

## Currency

WRLD-001 establishes Vyra as the official national currency of Alamat. "Vyra" functions as both singular and plural.

HIS-001 establishes that Vyra traces its legitimacy to the Chikagoland Amethyst Crown. The currency, official seal, and national treasury bear the Amethyst as an enduring emblem. This symbol should communicate resilience, humility, compassion, hope, remembrance, and leadership through service rather than wealth.

Alamat operates as a modern fantasy society. Citizens may use Vyra through physical currency, banking institutions, digital payments, salaries, military payroll, commercial trade, taxation, and everyday purchases.

Currency should reflect maritime trade, local trust, material scarcity, political authority, and barter traditions.

Possible economic forms:

- Port-issued stamped coins or trade weights.
- Shell, bead, metal, or ceramic tokens used in specific regions.
- Written debt tallies maintained by guilds, elders, or port officials.
- Barter through rice, salt, cloth, tools, medicine, or boat repair.
- Ritual gifts that function socially but not legally as payment.

Currency design rule: Vyra should feel familiar to modern players while existing naturally inside Alamat's fantasy world, history, and national symbolism.

## Education

Education should vary by region, class, craft, religion, and family role.

Education may include:

- Family apprenticeship.
- Boat navigation taught through stars, reefs, songs, and tide memory.
- Shrine instruction in ritual, etiquette, stories, and taboo management.
- Market arithmetic taught through trade.
- Craft guild training for weaving, carpentry, metalwork, pottery, map copying, and boatbuilding.
- Oral history taught through elders, songs, riddles, and repeated warnings.
- Limited formal schooling in larger settlements or archive towns.

Literacy should not be uniform. Some people may read trade marks but not old script; others may know songs that preserve meanings scholars cannot translate.

## Natural World

The natural world should be beautiful, useful, and dangerous.

### Climate

- Wet and dry seasonal rhythms.
- Typhoons and sudden sea changes.
- Mist-heavy mountain mornings.
- Humid lowland heat.
- Volcanic ashfall in specific regions.

### Flora

- Bamboo groves, mangroves, hardwood forests, medicinal herbs, flowering vines, rice terraces, fruit trees, moss-covered ruin growth, and plants associated with offerings or warding.

### Fauna

- Fish, birds, monitor lizards, bats, insects, water buffalo-like draft animals, reef creatures, mountain predators, and folklore-touched animals whose behavior locals read as omens.

See `Natural World.md`.

## Wildlife and Monsters

Wildlife and monsters should exist on a spectrum rather than in separate moral categories.

| Category | World Function |
| --- | --- |
| Ordinary Wildlife | Food, labor, pests, omens, pets, trade goods, ecosystem texture. |
| Dangerous Wildlife | Travel threat, village worry, hunting culture, regional fear. |
| Folklore-Touched Animals | Creatures whose behavior is interpreted through belief, taboo, or old agreements. |
| Spirits and Beings | Not automatically evil; may protect, punish, bargain, mislead, mourn, or simply exist. |
| Dungeon Creatures | Ecologically and historically tied to the dungeon rather than placed as generic enemies. |

Monster design must connect to habitat, legend, ecology, human relationship, and visual identity. No creature should exist only to fill an encounter table.

Climate should shape architecture, clothing, economy, travel, food storage, festivals, dungeon access, and local belief. Storms, flood cycles, dry seasons, volcanic activity, reef tides, and mountain mist should affect how people live and how players explore.

## Transportation

Travel should be a central part of world identity.

- Outrigger boats for coastal travel.
- River craft for interior routes.
- Rope bridges and mountain stairs.
- Footpaths through terraces and forests.
- Ferries controlled by local families.
- Hidden channels through mangroves.
- Ancient causeways that appear only at low tide or under specific conditions.

See `Transportation.md`.

## Technology

Technology should support Alamat as a modern fantasy society while remaining shaped by available materials, climate, trade, inherited craft, national institutions, and ancient discoveries. Modern convenience may exist in banking and daily life, but it should not erase adventure, travel, danger, or local texture.

See `Technology.md`.

### Technology Level

The baseline technology level is modern fantasy, with regional variation and strong craft traditions.

Common local and traditional technologies may include:

- Boats, outriggers, ferries, rope bridges, pulleys, carts, pack animals, hand tools, looms, kilns, forges, mills, rainwater systems, irrigation, stone terraces, storm shutters, oil lamps, lanterns, paper, maps, carved markers, and simple mechanical locks.

Modern civic and economic technologies may include banking institutions, salaries, military payroll systems, digital payments, commercial records, and treasury-backed financial infrastructure.

Rare or restricted ancient technologies may include:

- Ancient doors, water systems, astronomical devices, sealed mechanisms, impossible alignments, resonant stones, unusual metals, and ritual machinery whose original purpose is debated.

Technology should create wonder through craft, history, and mystery rather than modern convenience.

## Magic

Magic should feel native to the world, its cultures, its folklore, and its relationship with memory, spirits, nature, ancient civilizations, and ritual practice. It should remain readable as a JRPG system while having social and spiritual consequences in-world.

See `World Magic.md` and `../05_Gameplay/Magic.md`.

## Magic Traditions

Final player-facing magic systems remain under `../05_Gameplay/Magic.md`. AC-200 governs cultural and world logic.

Magic traditions may include:

| Tradition Type | World Role | Flexibility Note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Household Practices | Small protective habits, blessings, offerings, remedies, and taboos. | May be common without proving all magic is mechanically reliable. |
| Shrine Rituals | Community rites, seasonal protection, memorial practices, healing, warnings. | Specific rituals require later approval. |
| Navigator Lore | Weather reading, route memory, reef signs, star knowledge, song-coded travel. | May be practical knowledge, magic, or both depending on region. |
| Healer Traditions | Herbs, touch, prayer, memory, spirit negotiation, practical medicine. | Avoid defining a generic healer class. |
| Ruin Practices | Attempts to interpret or activate ancient sites. | Should remain dangerous, partial, and historically uncertain. |
| Creature Agreements | Local customs around spirits, beings, animals, and forbidden places. | Not every agreement is true, but every agreement affects behavior. |

Magic should create obligations. If magic helps people, it should also require responsibility, cost, permission, risk, taboo, training, or social consequence.
